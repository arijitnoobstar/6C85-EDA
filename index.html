Exploring the Dataset

This dataset initially contains about 49,000 entries of housing sale transactions in Boston over the last 24 years. The dataset contains a lot of information on the characteristics of each sales transaction that include investor data and pricing data. To explore the dataset, we analyze the spread of variables among all columns to 'gut-check' and see if their entries make sense. What I found immediately was that there are some nonsensical entries which we must filter out. The first thing I did was to filter out property prices within an acceptable range. I included homes that cost between $100,000 and $3,650,000. The upper bound was so that we can analyze the topic of speculation that does not include the ultra-wealthy. That would fit more towards the "housing for rich" subtheme. In this analysis, I am interested to see How investor activity differs from that of non-investors in the more affordable price range (I say this while acknowledging a 3.65 mil house falls a bit too far from my affordable range). The upper limit removes the top 5% of entries. The next thing I did was to remove data entries with no lat/long coordinates as I could not visualze them on a map. I then did a further preliminary analysis by visualization.

Outliers and Anomalies

The preliminary examination of the data revealed several outliers in key variables of interest, such as 'bathrooms', 'mortgage', 'lotsize', and 'totrooms'. Outliers can skew the results and may not be representative of the dataset as a whole.

Boxplot Analysis

To identify and understand the nature of these outliers, boxplots were generated for each of these variables. The boxplots highlighted data points that were significantly distant from the rest of the data distribution, signaling the need for further inspection.

For 'bathrooms' and 'totrooms', values that were beyond the typical range for residential properties were removed, as they were likely to be data entry errors or properties not relevant to the typical homebuyer. Anything with more than 8 total rooms or 4 bathrooms were filtered out.

The 'mortgage' variable contained some extremely high values, potentially indicating data entry errors. These were trimmed to include only the range consistent with residential financing. The image above shows clear outliers that need to be removed.

he 'lotsize' variable also had outliers that could represent large inaccuracies. These were adjusted to focus on typical residential property sizes. The image above clearly shows that there are outliers for lot sizes which were removed.

The resulting cleaned variables exhibited a much tighter distribution, which could then be used for more accurate analysis. This process not only improved the dataset's overall quality but also ensured that subsequent insights would be based on reliable and relevant data.

The heatmap above shows the general region of the property sales. The gut check shows that the properties are indeed spread around the Boston area. One interesting observation is that most of the purchases happen in the central part of Boston (Back Bay to Beacon Hill, to South End, North End, Financial District etc.). The home prices are also higher in these areas than the prices in the other districts of Boston, liek Dorchester or Mattapan etc. This might reflect either a bias in the data collection or property transactions or flipping happens more often in the central Boston area. We are now in a good shape to explore the questions posed.
